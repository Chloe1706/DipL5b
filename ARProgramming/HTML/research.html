Smartify

Image Tracking AR

Screen recording of Smartify app in use. Showcasing Home page, previous scans and settings menu.

Background image: Sainsbury Centre Living Area Collection

About

Smartify is an app which allows users to engage more deeply with art exhibitions through the use of AR. I had the opportunity to test this app myself at the Sainsbury centre’s Living Area collection.

By allowing the app to deploy the phone's rear camera, you can scan artworks and listen to audio descriptions or read about the artwork as well as context behind the artist, their process and influences. After making an account, the app will also store this information for users to revisit at another time.

Maintaining Curatorial Integrity

Many galleries have employed Smatify to enhance visitor engagement however, the Sainsbury centre has truly integrated this technology as it lends itself well to their curatorial style. The Living Area collection, sometimes referred to as ‘Living Art’ is a showcase of the extensive collection belonging to Robert and Lisa Sainsbury; avid collectors and patriots to the arts. The permanent installation is curated with a holistic approach to resemble a living room where visitors are invited to explore the work in a fluid environment where objects from different cultures, time periods and materials are displayed together without extensive labelling. This cultivates an experience adjacent to at one time walking around the home of Robert and Lisa. The space feels grounded and intimate as if the work still adorned the walls and surfaces of a living room.

In this instance, Smartify has empowered the curators to maintain a space uninterrupted by any visual clutter from labelling while still enabling visitors to access contextual information.

Additionally, the app encourages a personalised pace of engagement where the user gets autonomy around how they choose to explore the exhibit; the order, the pace etc. This is something that could not be achieved by simply offering guided tours. The integration of technology in this manner makes leaps in progress towards more accessible experiences.

Accessibility

I personally believe that as an emergent technology, AR lends itself to accessibility better than most. The fact that users need only a reasonably up to date smart phone to engage with it places it at a more accessible price point than other forms of extended reality which require more specialist equipment. Additionally, as an AR software which utilises image-tracking/marker-based AR, the app benefits from having an intuitive and familiar interface (smart phone camera); increasing usability across different audiences.

Beyond this, the app offers accessibility benefits that surpass standard museum experiences. With their collaboration with Smartify, the Sainsbury Centre now offers audio tours, converting visual artwork into audio descriptions to support visually impaired visitors. By opening the settings within the app, the user can also choose between a wide range of languages, even BSL and ASL, offering broad multilingual access that would not be viable in standard museum labelling or guided tours.

Finally, I think that in some instances the app could support visitors with reduced mobility by reducing the need to crowd around small labels or keep pace with a guided tour, empowering individuals to access the information from a comfortable viewing distance at a pace that suits them.

Feedback

Upon looking at its reviews on the App store, I can surmise that Smartify has been well-received by its users, receiving a 4.6 out of 5 star rating. Some have left reviews complaining that the app incentivises users to stare at their phones rather than actively look at and engage with the artwork in front of them. While I am sympathetic to this mindset, I do think the insinuation that visitors are less present is misguided given the aforementioned benefits to accessibility and overall engagement.

One issue I personally have with the app is how the homescreen prioritises upcoming exhibition and featured content, placing promotional material and advertising high in the visual hierarchy. I know that this likely reflects partnerships with museums and financial incentives. While on one hand, I can imagine that this supports cultural institutions and may increase user engagement with art; however, I think that prioritising the AR scan function would make the app more intuitive to navigate and reinforce its primary purpose.

Despite this, I think that the AR function itself works really well and positive feedback from users seems to affirm this.

Overall, I think Smartify is a great example of how AR can be integrated within historical institutions and potentially forecasts a future which serves to democratise access to art and deepen overall audience engagement.

19 Crimes

Web Marker-Based AR

Screen recording of AR in use. Demonstrating animated overlay on bottle label.

Background image: Assortment from 19 Crimes

About

19 Crimes is a wine company whose brand identity places significant emphasis on image tracking AR technology. The packaging features 18th century British criminals who were sentenced to ‘Transportation’ to Australia for committing any of 19 specific offences. Those who survived the journey would later be pivotal in pioneering and developing the early Australian colonies. Through Image tracking AR, the figures on the bottles animate and narrate their own stories when scanned with a smartphone.

Responding to Evolving AR Technology

Although first introduced in 2017, 19 Crimes has continued to develop their AR in line with the continued development and expansion of AR technology, positioning them as a relevant contemporary example of how AR is evolving past novelty, and becoming embedded into everyday commercial packaging. Upon their launch in 2017, 19 Crimes initially partnered with Living Wine Labels; a marker-based AR app developed by Treasury Wine Estates which seeked to capitalise on the recent success and notoriety of Augmented Reality.

Around 2020-2021 19 Crimes began transitioning into web-based AR, releasing labels which allowed the user to view the AR through their mobile web browser, without the need to download an app. This shift demonstrated the brands commitment to responding to evolving technology and recognising the value of easier access and reduced friction for users. As far as I can tell, they are the first of the brands partnered with Living Wine Labels to do this.

Audience and Accessibility

19 Crimes market themselves as a fairly affordable wine. Bottles retail between £8-£11, positioning them as an accessible option for a broad audience. Additionally, the brand's core identity centres on rebellion and historical storytelling, engaging consumers who may otherwise feel isolated by traditional wine branding and technical jargon. This cultivates an identity that resonates with consumers drawn to engagement-focused branding.

Industry analysts have identified that the brand is particularly successful with millennial consumers, a demographic that reportedly values authenticity, storytelling and shareable experiences. It is clear the brand targets younger, and on average, more digitally engaged consumers.

In the previous section I explored accessibility in conjunction with AR and how galleries using Smartify leverage the technology to engage broader audiences. When discussing 19 Crimes, the conversation around accessibility differs whereby it shifts from physical into cultural and emotional accessibility. What I mean by this is that by replacing traditional wine elitism with narrative-led branding and interactive storytelling, the brand lowers cultural barriers to participation. The marker-based AR feature serves to strengthen this accessibility by emphasising entertainment value and connection; transforming wine from a specialist commodity into an engaging and inclusive experience.

Feedback

Having tried out the AR for myself I found that it functions fairly well. I accessed the AR through my phone's browser by scanning a QR code on the bottles label. This is definitely preferable to downloading an app as the experience is more immediate and casual which I think aligns with the intent of the product.

It's clear that the developers were aiming for subtlety, where the face on the label animates without much disturbance to the rest of the label. Online, many consumers have likened the effect to the portraits in Harry Potter. I myself wonder if this contributes to its appeal to millennials.

I personally found myself wishing there had been more information around the individual on the bottle. I think the experience may have been enriched if after scanning and interacting with the AR there was a prompt to ‘find out more’ where the user could navigate to a page which went into more detail. That being said, I can’t really fault the AR itself. It consistently functioned as intended across multiple tries.

19 Crimes illustrates how augmented reality can transform everyday consumer products into interactive storytelling platforms and demonstrates how brands can utilise the technology to stand out in saturated markets and appeal to different audiences. Moreover, this reaffirms that AR is increasingly becoming embedded within everyday design, positioning it as a branding and design tool, rather than a mere short term novelty.
